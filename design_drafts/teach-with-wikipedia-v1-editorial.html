§ 01 · How it works

A program built by staff who have seen thousands of classrooms.

Our staff has been supporting the Wikipedia Education Program in the U.S. and Canada since 2010. We've taken the patterns from instructors who've succeeded — and the pitfalls from those who haven't — and built a program designed to make this assignment work in real classrooms.

You'll receive scaffolded training modules for your students, a dashboard for tracking their contributions, sample syllabi, and direct access to our staff throughout the term. We recommend a six- to twelve-week timeline for non-medical courses, with shorter timelines possible for medical writing assignments where students contribute via WikiProject Medicine's review process.

Students develop concrete, transferable skills: how to read a citation network, how to weigh source reliability, how to write in a neutral register for an audience that doesn't share your assumptions, and how to collaborate publicly with strangers. They draft their contributions themselves — without AI — to improve public access to high-quality, verifiable information.

For class sizes, we recommend individual articles for courses under 40 students, pairs or groups for 40–100 students, and discourage the assignment for courses over 100 students unless it's adapted into a structured group project.

The assignment is free for instructors and their students, funded by generous donations and grants. Our eligibility extends to instructors teaching at postsecondary institutions accredited in the United States and Canada.

§ 05 · Frequently asked

Questions, answered.

What is the Wikipedia assignment?

The Wikipedia assignment is a structured project in which your students improve Wikipedia articles in your course's subject area. They learn to navigate Wikipedia's policies, evaluate sources, and write for a public audience — and their work remains live online long after the term ends.

Who is eligible for free support?

Free support extends to instructors teaching at postsecondary institutions accredited in the United States and Canada. There are no eligibility restrictions based on discipline.

What do students learn?
  • Research and source evaluation
  • Writing for a public audience
  • Collaboration with peers and the broader Wikipedia community
  • AI literacy and a critical eye for verifiable information
How long should the assignment run?

We recommend a six- to twelve-week timeline for most courses. Medical writing courses contributing through WikiProject Medicine's review process are exempt from that minimum.

What class size works best?

Individual article assignments work well for classes under 40 students. Pairs or groups work for classes of 40–100. We don't recommend the assignment for classes over 100 unless adapted into a structured group project.

Can students use AI tools?

No. Students may not use AI tools to write or edit their text for Wikipedia. The goal of the assignment is for students to draft their contributions themselves to improve public access to high-quality, verifiable information.

How does my student protect their privacy?

Students choose their own Wikipedia usernames and are not required to use their real names. We provide guidance on selecting an appropriate username during training.

Can I run the assignment in multiple courses?

Yes. Once approved, you can run the assignment in as many courses as you like, each term, with our continued support.

How do I apply?

Visit the Wiki Education Dashboard to create your course page. Our staff reviews applications on a rolling basis and most instructors hear back within a few business days. Questions? Email studentprogram@wikiedu.org.
